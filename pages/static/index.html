Your Decentralized Homepage¶

Do you use Github Pages or S3 to host a simple webpage and love how easy it is? Have you ever added a webpage to IPFS and realized how powerful it could be? Textile Pages now brings the simplicity of S3 and the benefits of IPFS together, offering you a simple, reliable method to host your webpage on IPFS.

Textile Pages creates a live folder on your computer to host your webpage. Changes in that folder will automatically get posted to IPFS — Textile ensures that the latest version of your website remains available online even if your computer isn’t. Cool, right?

Setup¶

Start by running the Textile Desktop app. Every account you add to the Desktop app will automatically have a Textile Pages instance enabled.

The Pages app will automatically update your homepage when you change it's content as long as Textile is running and you are signed into your account.

Warning

Any changes you make in your pages folder will automatically be published to IPFS and potentially cached or mirrored on external IPFS peers. Do not add private, sensitive, or illegal content to your pages folder.

Website editing¶

This is your current default webpage. To update it, your Textile account directory,

~/Library/Application Support/Textile/<account>/

The pages app creates a folder here called, pages in which you will find the index.html folder containing the current index.html. That file is your default homepage.

You can open the index.html in you IDE of choice and begin editing.

Building React, Jekyll, or other websites¶

Update your current website's build steps to use the Pages pages folder as the target directory to build your website into.

Publishing updates¶

Textile should automatically detect changes in your pages folder and publish the results. As a response, you should get a new IPFS hash for your website, viewable through any public gateway.

https://gateway.textile.io/ipfs/<hash>

Cloudflare DNS¶

Textile Pages works seamlessly with Cloudflare's DNSLink support, allowing you to publish changes to your webpage over IPFS that are also available at domain names you control.

Cloudflare setup¶

For Textile Pages to automatically update your website, you must enable IPFS in your Cloudflare account manually first.

Read Cloudflare Instructions Here.

Specifically, your DNS records must contain the CNAME and TXT records for serving an IPFS hash. Next, you'll setup Textile Pages to update those records with your latest webpage update.

Cloudflare account credentials¶

You need to add four new environmental variables to the computer running Textile Pages.

PAGES_CLOUDFLARE_API_KEY

The API Key of your Cloudflare account.

export PAGES_CLOUDFLARE_API_KEY=<your-cloudflare-api-key>

PAGES_CLOUDFLARE_EMAIL

Your admin email address associated with the API key.

export PAGES_CLOUDFLARE_EMAIL=<your-cloudflare-api-key>

PAGES_DOMAIN_NAME

The top level domain name where your DNS records exist.

export PAGES_DOMAIN_NAME=<e.g. textile.io>

PAGES_IPFS_RECORD

The exact record subdomain and domain you will be updating. For example, it may be the same as PAGES_DOMAIN_NAME if you are updating your website's root page, or it may be somesubdomain.PAGES_DOMAIN_NAME if you are targetting a subdomain on your site.

export PAGES_IPFS_RECORD=<e.g. demo.textile.io>

Updating Cloudflare¶

If you have set your ENV correctly, the next time you update your index.html a new IPFS has will be generated and your Cloudflare DNS will be updated.

Post update script¶

Cloudflare DNS updates are performed using a post-update script. You can modify this script to manage your Cloudflare updates differently (or perform entirely new tasks).

Find the post update script in

~/Library/Application Support/Textile/<account>/scripts/pages-post-script-<v>.sh
